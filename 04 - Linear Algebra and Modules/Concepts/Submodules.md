# Submodules

## Definition

> [!info] Definition (Submodule)
> Let $M$ be an $R$-[[04 - Linear Algebra and Modules/Concepts/Module Definition|module]]. A subset $N \subseteq M$ is a **submodule** if $N$ is itself an $R$-module under the same operations.

> [!tip] Submodule Test
> A non-empty subset $N \subseteq M$ is a submodule if and only if:
> 1. $n_1 + n_2 \in N$ for all $n_1, n_2 \in N$
> 2. $rn \in N$ for all $r \in R$, $n \in N$
>
> Equivalently: $r_1 n_1 + r_2 n_2 \in N$ for all $r_1, r_2 \in R$ and $n_1, n_2 \in N$.

## Key Properties

1. Every submodule contains $0$
2. The intersection of submodules is a submodule
3. $\{0\}$ and $M$ are always submodules (trivial submodules)
4. For $R$-module $R$: submodules of $R$ are precisely the [[02 - Ring Theory/Concepts/Ideals|ideals]] of $R$

## Operations on Submodules

> [!info] Definition (Sum of Submodules)
> For submodules $N_1, N_2 \subseteq M$:
> $$N_1 + N_2 = \{n_1 + n_2 : n_1 \in N_1, n_2 \in N_2\}$$

> [!info] Definition (Submodule Generated by a Set)
> For $S \subseteq M$:
> $$\langle S \rangle = RS = \left\{\sum_{i=1}^n r_i s_i : r_i \in R, s_i \in S, n \in \mathbb{N}\right\}$$

## Comparison: Subspaces vs Submodules

| [[04 - Linear Algebra and Modules/Concepts/Subspaces|Subspaces]] (Vector Spaces) | Submodules (Modules) |
|---------------------------|----------------------|
| Always have complement | May not have complement |
| Direct sum decomposition exists | Not always |
| Dimension well-defined | Rank (only for free modules) |

## Examples

> [!example] Example 1: Ideals as submodules
> Any ideal $I \subseteq R$ is a submodule of $R$ as an $R$-module.

> [!example] Example 2: $\mathbb{Z}$-modules
> Submodules of $\mathbb{Z}^n$ are the subgroups (since $\mathbb{Z}$-modules = abelian groups).

> [!example] Example 3: Torsion submodule
> $T(M) = \{m \in M : rm = 0 \text{ for some } r \neq 0\}$ is a submodule.

> [!example] Example 4: Kernel and image
> For [[04 - Linear Algebra and Modules/Concepts/Module Homomorphisms|homomorphism]] $f: M \to N$:
> - $\ker(f)$ is a submodule of $M$
> - $\text{im}(f)$ is a submodule of $N$

## Lattice of Submodules

The set of all submodules of $M$ forms a lattice under:
- Meet: $N_1 \wedge N_2 = N_1 \cap N_2$
- Join: $N_1 \vee N_2 = N_1 + N_2$
