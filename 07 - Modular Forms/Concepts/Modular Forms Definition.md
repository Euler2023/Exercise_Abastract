# Modular Forms Definition

## Definition

> [!info] Definition (Modular Form)
> A **modular form** of weight $k$ for $\text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ is a function $f: \mathbb{H} \to \mathbb{C}$ satisfying:
>
> 1. **Holomorphicity**: $f$ is holomorphic on $\mathbb{H}$
>
> 2. **Modularity**: For all $\gamma = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \in \text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$:
> $$f\left(\frac{a\tau + b}{c\tau + d}\right) = (c\tau + d)^k f(\tau)$$
>
> 3. **Holomorphicity at cusps**: $f$ is holomorphic at $i\infty$ (and all cusps)

## Modularity Condition Explained

The modularity condition can be written as:
$$f(\gamma \cdot \tau) = j(\gamma, \tau)^k f(\tau)$$
where $j(\gamma, \tau) = c\tau + d$ is the **automorphy factor**.

For the generators:
- $f(\tau + 1) = f(\tau)$ (from $T$)
- $f(-1/\tau) = \tau^k f(\tau)$ (from $S$)

## Fourier Expansion

> [!info] Definition (q-expansion)
> Since $f(\tau + 1) = f(\tau)$, we can write:
> $$f(\tau) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n q^n$$
> where $q = e^{2\pi i \tau}$.
>
> The coefficients $a_n$ are the **Fourier coefficients** of $f$.

> [!tip] Holomorphicity at $\infty$
> "Holomorphic at $i\infty$" means the $q$-expansion has no negative powers of $q$ (i.e., $a_n = 0$ for $n < 0$).

## Space of Modular Forms

> [!info] Definition (Space $M_k$)
> $$M_k = M_k(\text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}))$$
> denotes the $\mathbb{C}$-vector space of modular forms of weight $k$.

Key facts:
- $M_k = \{0\}$ for $k$ odd or $k < 0$
- $M_0 = \mathbb{C}$ (constants)
- $M_2 = \{0\}$
- $\dim M_k < \infty$ for all $k$

## Dimension Formula

> [!abstract] Theorem (Dimension Formula)
> For $k \geq 0$ even:
> $$\dim M_k = \begin{cases}
> \lfloor k/12 \rfloor & \text{if } k \equiv 2 \pmod{12} \\
> \lfloor k/12 \rfloor + 1 & \text{otherwise}
> \end{cases}$$

| $k$ | 0 | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 | 10 | 12 | 14 | 16 |
|-----|---|---|---|---|---|----|----|----|----|
| $\dim M_k$ | 1 | 0 | 1 | 1 | 1 | 1 | 2 | 1 | 2 |

## Graded Ring Structure

> [!abstract] Theorem
> The graded ring of modular forms is a polynomial algebra:
> $$M_* = \bigoplus_{k \geq 0} M_k = \mathbb{C}[E_4, E_6]$$
> generated by [[07 - Modular Forms/Concepts/Eisenstein Series|Eisenstein series]] $E_4$ and $E_6$.

## Examples

> [!example] Example 1: Constant function
> $f(\tau) = 1$ is a modular form of weight 0.

> [!example] Example 2: Eisenstein series
> $E_4(\tau)$ and $E_6(\tau)$ are modular forms of weights 4 and 6.

> [!example] Example 3: Discriminant
> $\Delta(\tau) = q \prod_{n=1}^{\infty}(1-q^n)^{24}$ is a modular form of weight 12.

## Weakly Modular Forms

> [!info] Definition (Weakly Modular)
> A function satisfying (1) and (2) but only **meromorphic** at cusps is called **weakly modular**.
