# Exercise Rep5: sl₂ Representation Classification

## Problem Statement

> [!question] Problem
> Let $\mathfrak{sl}_2(\mathbb{C})$ have the standard basis $\{e, f, h\}$ with relations:
> $$[h, e] = 2e, \quad [h, f] = -2f, \quad [e, f] = h$$
>
> **(a)** Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-representation. Prove that:
> - The eigenvalues of $h$ are integers
> - If $v$ is an eigenvector of $h$ with eigenvalue $\lambda$, then $e \cdot v$ (if nonzero) has eigenvalue $\lambda + 2$
>
> **(b)** Prove that every finite-dimensional irreducible representation has a **highest weight vector**: a vector $v_0$ with $e \cdot v_0 = 0$ and $h \cdot v_0 = n \cdot v_0$ for some $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$.
>
> **(c)** For the irreducible representation $V_n$ with highest weight $n-1$, explicitly describe:
> - A basis $\{v_0, v_1, \ldots, v_{n-1}\}$
> - The action of $e$, $f$, $h$ on this basis
>
> **(d)** Verify that $V_4$ (4-dimensional irreducible) satisfies $\dim V_4 = 4$ using the Casimir operator.

## Hints

> [!hint]- Hint 1 (Part a)
> Use the relation $[h, e] = 2e$. If $h \cdot v = \lambda v$, compute $h \cdot (e \cdot v)$.

> [!hint]- Hint 2 (Part b)
> Consider the eigenvalues of $h$. Since $V$ is finite-dimensional, there are finitely many. What happens when you keep applying $e$?

> [!hint]- Hint 3 (Part c)
> Start with $v_0$ (highest weight) and define $v_k = f^k \cdot v_0$. Use the commutation relations to find $e \cdot v_k$.

> [!hint]- Hint 4 (Part d)
> The Casimir operator $\Omega = 2ef + h + \frac{h^2}{2}$ acts on $V_n$ by the scalar $\frac{(n-1)(n+1)}{2}$.

## Solution

> [!success]- Solution
>
> **(a) Integer eigenvalues and weight shifting**
>
> Let $h \cdot v = \lambda v$. Compute:
> $$h \cdot (e \cdot v) = [h, e] \cdot v + e \cdot (h \cdot v) = 2e \cdot v + \lambda (e \cdot v) = (\lambda + 2)(e \cdot v)$$
>
> So $e$ raises the eigenvalue by 2. Similarly, $f$ lowers it by 2.
>
> Since $V$ is finite-dimensional, the set of eigenvalues is finite. Starting from any eigenvalue and repeatedly adding 2 must eventually give 0 (eigenvector killed by $e$). Similarly going down. The eigenvalues form a string: $\{n, n-2, \ldots, -n+2, -n\}$ for some $n$. Since this is symmetric about 0, all eigenvalues are integers.
>
> ---
>
> **(b) Existence of highest weight vector**
>
> The eigenvalues of $h$ form a finite set of integers. Let $\lambda_{\max}$ be the largest. Take $v_0 \in V_{\lambda_{\max}}$.
>
> Then $e \cdot v_0$ has eigenvalue $\lambda_{\max} + 2 > \lambda_{\max}$. Since $\lambda_{\max}$ is maximal, we must have $e \cdot v_0 = 0$.
>
> So $v_0$ is a highest weight vector with $h \cdot v_0 = \lambda_{\max} v_0$.
>
> For $V$ irreducible: $V$ is generated by $v_0$ under the action of $\mathfrak{sl}_2$, which means $V = \text{span}\{v_0, f \cdot v_0, f^2 \cdot v_0, \ldots\}$.
>
> The series must terminate: $f^n \cdot v_0 \neq 0$ but $f^{n+1} \cdot v_0 = 0$ for some $n$. One can show $\lambda_{\max} = n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$.
>
> ---
>
> **(c) Explicit structure of $V_n$**
>
> Let $v_0$ be the highest weight vector with $h \cdot v_0 = (n-1) v_0$.
> Define $v_k = f^k \cdot v_0$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$.
>
> **Action of $h$:**
> $$h \cdot v_k = h \cdot f^k \cdot v_0 = (n-1-2k) v_k$$
>
> **Action of $f$:**
> $$f \cdot v_k = v_{k+1} \quad (v_n = 0)$$
>
> **Action of $e$:**
> We compute using $[e, f] = h$:
> $$e \cdot v_k = e \cdot f \cdot v_{k-1} = (f \cdot e + h) \cdot v_{k-1} = f \cdot (e \cdot v_{k-1}) + (n-1-2(k-1)) v_{k-1}$$
>
> By induction, $e \cdot v_k = k(n-k) v_{k-1}$.
>
> **Summary:**
> - $h \cdot v_k = (n-1-2k) v_k$
> - $f \cdot v_k = v_{k+1}$
> - $e \cdot v_k = k(n-k) v_{k-1}$
> - $e \cdot v_0 = 0$, $f \cdot v_{n-1} = 0$
>
> ---
>
> **(d) Verification using Casimir**
>
> For $V_4$, highest weight is $n-1 = 3$ (so $n = 4$).
>
> The Casimir $\Omega = 2ef + h + \frac{h^2}{2}$ acts by:
> $$\Omega|_{V_n} = \frac{(n-1)(n+1)}{2} = \frac{n^2 - 1}{2}$$
>
> For $n = 4$: $\Omega = \frac{16 - 1}{2} = \frac{15}{2}$.
>
> Let's verify on $v_0$ (weight 3):
> - $e \cdot v_0 = 0$, so $ef \cdot v_0 = e \cdot v_1 = 1 \cdot (4-1) v_0 = 3v_0$
> - $h \cdot v_0 = 3v_0$
> - $h^2 \cdot v_0 = 9v_0$
>
> $$\Omega \cdot v_0 = 2(3) + 3 + \frac{9}{2} = 6 + 3 + 4.5 = 13.5 = \frac{15}{2} \ ✓$$
>
> This confirms $\dim V_4 = 4$ (the representation with Casimir eigenvalue $\frac{15}{2}$).
