# Kac-Moody Algebras

## Introduction

Kac-Moody algebras generalize finite-dimensional semisimple Lie algebras to infinite dimensions. They were independently discovered by Victor Kac and Robert Moody in 1968.

## Generalized Cartan Matrix

> [!info] Definition (Generalized Cartan Matrix)
> A **generalized Cartan matrix** (GCM) is an $n \times n$ integer matrix $A = (a_{ij})$ satisfying:
>
> 1. $a_{ii} = 2$ for all $i$
> 2. $a_{ij} \leq 0$ for $i \neq j$
> 3. $a_{ij} = 0 \Leftrightarrow a_{ji} = 0$

A GCM is **symmetrizable** if there exists a diagonal matrix $D$ with positive entries such that $DA$ is symmetric.

## Definition of Kac-Moody Algebras

> [!info] Definition
> The **Kac-Moody algebra** $\mathfrak{g}(A)$ associated to a GCM $A$ is generated by:
> - $e_i, f_i$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$ (Chevalley generators)
> - $h_i$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$ (Cartan subalgebra generators)
>
> subject to the relations:
> 1. $[h_i, h_j] = 0$
> 2. $[h_i, e_j] = a_{ij} e_j$
> 3. $[h_i, f_j] = -a_{ij} f_j$
> 4. $[e_i, f_j] = \delta_{ij} h_i$
> 5. $(\text{ad } e_i)^{1-a_{ij}}(e_j) = 0$ for $i \neq j$ (Serre relations)
> 6. $(\text{ad } f_i)^{1-a_{ij}}(f_j) = 0$ for $i \neq j$ (Serre relations)

## Classification by Type

The nature of $\mathfrak{g}(A)$ depends critically on the properties of $A$:

> [!abstract] Classification
> Let $A$ be an indecomposable GCM. Then exactly one of the following holds:
>
> | Type | Condition | Dimension | Examples |
> |------|-----------|-----------|----------|
> | **Finite** | $A$ is positive definite | Finite | $A_n, B_n, C_n, D_n, E_6, E_7, E_8, F_4, G_2$ |
> | **Affine** | $A$ is positive semidefinite, $\det A = 0$ | Infinite (countable) | $\widehat{A}_n, \widehat{D}_n, \widehat{E}_6, \ldots$ |
> | **Indefinite** | $A$ is indefinite | Infinite | Hyperbolic, Lorentzian |

## Structure

### Triangular Decomposition

$$\mathfrak{g}(A) = \mathfrak{n}^- \oplus \mathfrak{h} \oplus \mathfrak{n}^+$$

where:
- $\mathfrak{h}$ is the Cartan subalgebra (spanned by $h_i$ and possibly additional elements)
- $\mathfrak{n}^+$ is generated by $e_i$
- $\mathfrak{n}^-$ is generated by $f_i$

### Root System

> [!info] Definition
> The **root system** $\Delta$ of $\mathfrak{g}(A)$ consists of:
> - **Real roots**: $\Delta^{re}$ (analogous to finite case, $W$-orbit of simple roots)
> - **Imaginary roots**: $\Delta^{im}$ (new phenomenon, can have multiplicities $> 1$)

For affine algebras, imaginary roots have the form $n\delta$ where $\delta$ is the **null root**.

### Weyl Group

The **Weyl group** $W$ is generated by simple reflections $s_i$ with relations:
$$s_i^2 = 1, \quad (s_i s_j)^{m_{ij}} = 1$$

where $m_{ij}$ depends on $a_{ij} a_{ji}$.

| $a_{ij} a_{ji}$ | $m_{ij}$ |
|-----------------|----------|
| 0 | 2 |
| 1 | 3 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 6 |
| $\geq 4$ | $\infty$ |

For affine type, $W$ is an **affine Weyl group** (infinite).

## Important Examples

### Finite Type (Classical)

When $A$ is a classical Cartan matrix, $\mathfrak{g}(A)$ is the corresponding finite-dimensional simple Lie algebra.

### Affine Type $\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}}_2$

The simplest affine Kac-Moody algebra:
$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -2 \\ -2 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

This is the **affine extension** of $\mathfrak{sl}_2$:
$$\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}}_2 = \mathfrak{sl}_2[t, t^{-1}] \oplus \mathbb{C}c \oplus \mathbb{C}d$$

where $c$ is the **central element** and $d$ is the **derivation**.

### Hyperbolic Type

GCM of signature $(n-1, 1)$. Example: rank 3 with
$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -2 & 0 \\ -2 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

## Representation Theory

### Highest Weight Modules

> [!info] Definition
> A **highest weight module** $L(\lambda)$ is generated by a vector $v_\lambda$ with:
> - $h \cdot v_\lambda = \lambda(h) v_\lambda$ for $h \in \mathfrak{h}$
> - $\mathfrak{n}^+ \cdot v_\lambda = 0$

### Integrable Modules

> [!info] Definition
> A module $V$ is **integrable** if:
> 1. $V = \bigoplus_{\mu} V_\mu$ (weight space decomposition)
> 2. $e_i$ and $f_i$ act locally nilpotently

For affine algebras, integrable highest weight modules are the natural generalizations of finite-dimensional representations.

### Weyl-Kac Character Formula

> [!abstract] Theorem (Weyl-Kac)
> For an integrable highest weight module $L(\lambda)$:
> $$\text{ch } L(\lambda) = \frac{\sum_{w \in W} \epsilon(w) e^{w(\lambda + \rho) - \rho}}{\prod_{\alpha \in \Delta^+} (1 - e^{-\alpha})^{\text{mult}(\alpha)}}$$

For affine algebras, this involves infinite products and sums.

### Denominator Identity

> [!abstract] Theorem (Macdonald-Kac)
> $$\prod_{\alpha \in \Delta^+} (1 - e^{-\alpha})^{\text{mult}(\alpha)} = \sum_{w \in W} \epsilon(w) e^{w(\rho) - \rho}$$

For affine $\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}}_2$, this gives the **Jacobi triple product identity**.

## Applications

### Mathematical Physics
- **Conformal Field Theory**: Affine algebras are the symmetry algebras
- **String Theory**: Kac-Moody symmetries in worldsheet theories
- **Integrable Systems**: Soliton equations have Kac-Moody symmetry

### Number Theory
- **Modular Forms**: Characters of affine modules are modular forms
- **Moonshine**: Connection to sporadic groups via indefinite Kac-Moody

### Combinatorics
- **Crystal Bases**: Kashiwara's theory
- **Macdonald Identities**: Special cases of denominator formula

## Connection to Loop Algebras

> [!info] Construction
> For a finite-dimensional simple Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$:
>
> The **loop algebra** is $\mathfrak{g}[t, t^{-1}] = \mathfrak{g} \otimes \mathbb{C}[t, t^{-1}]$.
>
> The **affine Kac-Moody algebra** is (roughly):
> $$\widehat{\mathfrak{g}} = \mathfrak{g}[t, t^{-1}] \oplus \mathbb{C}c \oplus \mathbb{C}d$$
>
> with central extension by $c$ and derivation $d = t\frac{d}{dt}$.
