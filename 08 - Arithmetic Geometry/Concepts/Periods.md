# Periods

## Definition

> [!info] Definition (Period)
> A **period** is a complex number obtained by integrating an algebraic differential form over an algebraic cycle. Formally, it is a number of the form:
> $$\int_\gamma \omega$$
> where $\omega$ is a closed algebraic differential form on a variety $X/\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$ and $\gamma$ is a cycle in $X(\mathbb{C})$.

Equivalently: periods are the matrix entries of the **comparison isomorphism** between de Rham and Betti cohomology.

## The Comparison Isomorphism

For a smooth projective variety $X/\mathbb{Q}$:

$$H^n_{\text{dR}}(X/\mathbb{Q}) \otimes_\mathbb{Q} \mathbb{C} \xrightarrow{\sim} H^n_B(X(\mathbb{C}), \mathbb{Q}) \otimes_\mathbb{Q} \mathbb{C}$$

| Cohomology | Coefficients | Structure |
|------------|--------------|-----------|
| de Rham $H^n_{\text{dR}}$ | $\mathbb{Q}$-vector space | Algebraic (Hodge filtration) |
| Betti $H^n_B$ | $\mathbb{Q}$-vector space | Topological |

The **period matrix** $P = (p_{ij})$ expresses a de Rham basis in terms of a Betti basis:
$$\omega_i = \sum_j p_{ij} \gamma_j$$

The entries $p_{ij}$ are **periods**.

## Classical Examples

> [!example] Example 1: $\pi$
> $$\pi = \int_{-1}^{1} \frac{dx}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} = \oint_{|z|=1} \frac{dz}{iz}$$
> This is the period of the circle $x^2 + y^2 = 1$.

> [!example] Example 2: $\log 2$
> $$\log 2 = \int_1^2 \frac{dx}{x}$$
> Period of $\mathbb{G}_m$ integrated over a path from 1 to 2.

> [!example] Example 3: Elliptic Periods
> For $E: y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$, the periods are:
> $$\omega_1 = \int_{\gamma_1} \frac{dx}{y}, \quad \omega_2 = \int_{\gamma_2} \frac{dx}{y}$$
> where $\gamma_1, \gamma_2$ form a basis of $H_1(E(\mathbb{C}), \mathbb{Z})$.
>
> The period lattice $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}\omega_1 + \mathbb{Z}\omega_2$ satisfies $E(\mathbb{C}) \cong \mathbb{C}/\Lambda$.

> [!example] Example 4: Euler's Beta Function
> $$B(a, b) = \int_0^1 x^{a-1}(1-x)^{b-1} dx = \frac{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a+b)}$$
> Periods of $\mathbb{P}^1 \setminus \{0, 1, \infty\}$.

> [!example] Example 5: Multiple Zeta Values
> $$\zeta(n_1, \ldots, n_k) = \sum_{0 < m_1 < \cdots < m_k} \frac{1}{m_1^{n_1} \cdots m_k^{n_k}}$$
> These are periods of mixed Tate motives over $\mathbb{Z}$.

## The Ring of Periods

> [!info] Definition
> The **ring of periods** $\mathcal{P}$ is the $\mathbb{Q}$-algebra generated by all periods.

$$\bar{\mathbb{Q}} \subset \mathcal{P} \subset \mathbb{C}$$

### What's in $\mathcal{P}$?

| In $\mathcal{P}$ | Not known to be in $\mathcal{P}$ |
|------------------|----------------------------------|
| $\pi$, $\log(n)$ for $n \in \mathbb{Q}$ | $e$ |
| $\zeta(n)$ for $n \geq 2$ | $1/\pi$ |
| Algebraic numbers | $e^\pi$ (but $e^{i\pi} = -1 \in \bar{\mathbb{Q}}$) |
| Elliptic periods | Euler's constant $\gamma$ |
| Multiple zeta values | |

## Period Conjectures

### Grothendieck's Period Conjecture

> [!abstract] Conjecture (Grothendieck)
> The only algebraic relations among periods come from geometry:
>
> Every polynomial relation $P(p_1, \ldots, p_n) = 0$ among periods arises from an algebraic cycle.

### Kontsevich-Zagier

> [!abstract] Conjecture (Kontsevich-Zagier)
> The ring $\mathcal{P}$ has a "good" structure: periods can be computed, and their relations understood via motivic considerations.

## Periods and L-functions

### Critical Values

For a motive $M$, the **critical values** of $L(M, s)$ are (conjecturally) algebraic multiples of periods.

> [!abstract] Deligne's Conjecture
> For a critical integer $n$:
> $$\frac{L(M, n)}{(2\pi i)^{d^+ n} \cdot c^+(M)} \in \bar{\mathbb{Q}}$$
> where $c^+(M)$ is a **period** of the motive $M$.

### Example: Riemann Zeta

$$\zeta(2n) = \frac{(-1)^{n+1} B_{2n} (2\pi)^{2n}}{2(2n)!}$$

Here $\zeta(2n) / \pi^{2n} \in \mathbb{Q}$, confirming Deligne's conjecture for $M = \mathbb{Q}(0)$.

## Periods in BSD

The **real period** $\Omega_E$ in BSD:
$$\Omega_E = \int_{E(\mathbb{R})} \omega = \begin{cases} \omega_1 & \text{if } E(\mathbb{R}) \text{ connected} \\ 2\omega_1 & \text{if } E(\mathbb{R}) \text{ has two components} \end{cases}$$

BSD predicts:
$$\frac{L(E, 1)}{\Omega_E} \in \mathbb{Q} \cdot (\text{arithmetic invariants})$$

## Period Domains

> [!info] Definition
> A **period domain** $D$ parametrizes Hodge structures of a given type. For weight $n$ and Hodge numbers $(h^{p,q})$:
> $$D = \{F^\bullet \in \text{Flag} : F^p \cap \bar{F}^{n-p+1} = 0\}$$

Variations of Hodge structure give **period maps** $\phi: S \to D/\Gamma$.

## Transcendence

> [!abstract] Theorem (Lindemann-Weierstrass)
> If $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n$ are algebraic and linearly independent over $\mathbb{Q}$, then $e^{\alpha_1}, \ldots, e^{\alpha_n}$ are algebraically independent.

> [!abstract] Theorem (Baker)
> If $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n$ are algebraic, then $\log \alpha_1, \ldots, \log \alpha_n$ are either all zero or linearly independent over $\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$.

These constrain relations among periods.
